FAQ

QA consulting — your questions answered

What does a QA consultant do day-to-day?

+

A QA consultant's day-to-day work depends on the phase of the engagement. In the first week, they audit your existing testing process — reviewing test coverage, tooling, defect history, and release procedures to identify the highest-risk gaps. From week two onwards, they shift into embedded mode: attending sprint standups, writing and executing test cases, triaging defects, setting up CI/CD quality gates, and producing release readiness sign-offs.

Unlike a testing-only contractor, a QA consultant also works on the process layer — designing workflows, writing test strategy documents, coaching developers on testability, and reporting quality metrics to engineering leadership. At goGreenlit, our consultants do all of this from day one, not in a phased "consulting then implementation" model.

What are the signs my engineering team needs a QA consultant?

+

The clearest signals that you need a QA consultant are: bugs reaching production on a regular basis, no consistent release checklist or sign-off process, developers testing their own code with no independent verification, and test coverage that hasn't kept pace with product growth.

You also likely need a QA consultant if: your CI/CD pipeline has no automated quality gates, your team has no documented test strategy, releases feel stressful and unpredictable, or you're preparing for a major launch or enterprise client and need to demonstrate quality rigor quickly. If two or more of these apply, it's worth a conversation — most teams we work with have been living with these problems for months before reaching out.

How is QA consulting different from simply hiring a QA tester?

+

A QA tester executes test cases — they find bugs, report them, and verify fixes. A QA consultant does that and designs the system around it: the test strategy, the defect lifecycle, the release criteria, the CI/CD quality gates, and the metrics that tell engineering leadership whether quality is improving or degrading over time.

Think of it this way: a tester runs the plays, a QA consultant designs the playbook. Most early-stage and scaling engineering teams are missing the playbook — they have testers (often developers) executing ad-hoc checks, but no governing quality framework. That's the gap goGreenlit fills. And unlike traditional consulting firms that write strategy documents and leave, we stay in the room and implement alongside your team every sprint.
